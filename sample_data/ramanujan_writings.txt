LETTERS TO G.H. HARDY
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Dear Sir, I beg to introduce myself to you as a clerk in the Accounts Department of the Port Trust Office at Madras on a salary of only £20 per annum. I am now about 23 years of age. I have had no University education but I have undergone the ordinary school course. After leaving school I have been employing the spare time at my disposal to work at Mathematics. I have not trodden through the conventional regular course which is followed in a University course, but I am striking out a new path for myself. I have made a special investigation of divergent series in general and the results I get are termed by the local mathematicians as "startling."
[Source: Letter to G.H. Hardy, 16 January 1913]

I have found a function which exactly represents the number of prime numbers less than x — the so-called "prime counting function." If I tell you this you will at once point out to me the lunacy of my claim, for the Prime Number Theorem is well known. But my method is entirely different, and I arrive at the result by a path that, so far as I know, no one has trodden before me.
[Source: Letter to G.H. Hardy, 16 January 1913]

Very recently I came across a tract by Hardy on "Orders of Infinity" and I found that I had anticipated many of the results proved there. I have found in Mr. Hardy a sympathetic colleague, and I hope to justify the interest he has taken in me. I shall write to you more about my results in later communications.
[Source: Letter to G.H. Hardy, 1913]

The series 1 + 2 + 3 + 4 + ... = −1/12. I told Mr. Hardy about this result, and he was quite taken aback by it. I know that it appears absurd on the surface, but there is a deep truth underlying this identity. When one uses the proper analytic continuation, the result is not merely formal — it carries real meaning. I have applied similar methods to many other divergent series with startling results.
[Source: Letter to G.H. Hardy, 1913]

I am very much gratified on perusing your letter of the 8th February 1913, and I am encouraged thereby to send a few more of my results. If you find any of them interesting, I shall be very happy. I have not given the proofs of many of these results, as the methods I employ are so different from the ordinary that I fear they may not be readily understood. I shall be very happy to explain the methods if you wish.
[Source: Letter to G.H. Hardy, 27 February 1913]

I beg to acknowledge receipt of your registered letter of the 26th February. I must apologize for the delay in replying. I find that the results I sent you in my last letter are all correct, and I have since obtained several new results of a similar nature. I am sending some of these with this letter. I have found the exact value of the integral of e^(−x^2) from 0 to infinity by a new method, and I have also found a remarkable continued fraction for the ratio of two such integrals.
[Source: Letter to G.H. Hardy, March 1913]

The numbers 2, 5, 15, 52, 203, 877 ... have a strange beauty to me. These are the Bell numbers, the number of ways to partition a set. I believe I have found a formula that gives their generating function in a form that Hardy sahib would find pleasing. Every sequence of numbers is, to me, like a river — you must follow it to its source.
[Source: Letter to G.H. Hardy, 1914]

I am quite well now, though the cold of Cambridge is very trying. I find the food here most disagreeable, and I miss the taste of rasam and sambar beyond what I can express. But the mathematics here — the libraries, the conversations with Hardy sahib — these make the hardship worthwhile. I have been working on the partition function and I believe I am close to an exact formula.
[Source: Letter to G.H. Hardy, 1915]

NOTEBOOK EXCERPTS AND MATHEMATICAL RESULTS
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The number of unrestricted partitions of n, which I denote p(n), grows with extraordinary rapidity. Consider: p(1) = 1, p(2) = 2, p(3) = 3, p(4) = 5, p(5) = 7, p(10) = 42, p(50) = 204226, p(100) = 190569292536. Hardy sahib and I discovered together an asymptotic formula: p(n) is approximately (1/(4n√3)) × exp(π√(2n/3)) as n grows large. This formula, which we proved rigorously, gives astonishingly accurate results even for moderate values of n.
[Source: Ramanujan's Notebook, Partition Theory, c. 1916]

I have discovered remarkable congruences for the partition function. For any non-negative integer n: p(5n + 4) is always divisible by 5, p(7n + 5) is always divisible by 7, and p(11n + 6) is always divisible by 11. These congruences are not accidents — they arise from deep symmetries in the theory of modular forms. I have found many more such congruences, but these three are the most beautiful.
[Source: Ramanujan's Notebook, Partition Congruences, c. 1917]

The continued fraction: 1/(1 + e^(−2π)/(1 + e^(−4π)/(1 + e^(−6π)/...))) equals (√(5+√5)/2 − (1+√5)/2) × e^(2π/5). This is one of the most beautiful identities I have found. When I first wrote it down, I sat for a long time in silence, feeling that I had been permitted to glimpse something sacred. The continued fraction converges with extraordinary rapidity.
[Source: Ramanujan's Notebook II, Continued Fractions, c. 1913]

My approximation for pi: (9801/(2206√2)) × Σ (4k)!(1103+26390k)/((k!)^4 × 396^(4k)) for k = 0, 1, 2, ... This series converges so rapidly that the first term alone gives pi correct to six decimal places. The second term gives eight more correct digits. I discovered this in Madras, working alone, and I still do not fully understand why 9801 and 26390 appear — they came to me, as so many things do, without a clear path of derivation.
[Source: Ramanujan's Notebook, Pi Approximations, c. 1914]

The Rogers-Ramanujan identities state that the sum of q^(n^2)/((1-q)(1-q^2)...(1-q^n)) for n = 0, 1, 2, ... equals the infinite product 1/((1-q)(1-q^4)(1-q^6)(1-q^9)(1-q^11)...) where the product runs over integers congruent to 1 or 4 modulo 5. I first found these identities empirically, by computing many terms and recognizing the pattern. Later I learned that Rogers had proved them in 1894, but by a method quite different from mine.
[Source: Ramanujan's Notebook, Rogers-Ramanujan Identities, c. 1913]

I define a highly composite number as a positive integer with more divisors than any smaller positive integer. The sequence begins: 1, 2, 4, 6, 12, 24, 36, 48, 60, 120, 180, 240, 360, 720, ... These numbers have a beauty that few have appreciated. I have proved that every highly composite number can be expressed as a product of primorial numbers in a specific way, and I have found asymptotic formulas for their distribution.
[Source: Ramanujan's Paper on Highly Composite Numbers, 1915]

The mock theta functions, which I wrote about in my last letter to Hardy in January 1920, are among my most mysterious discoveries. These are functions that behave like theta functions in some ways but not others. I gave seventeen examples, organized into groups that I call "third order," "fifth order," and "seventh order." I believe they are connected to deep structures in the theory of modular forms, but I have not been able to prove this fully. I wrote to Hardy: "I discovered very interesting functions recently which I call 'Mock' theta functions. Unlike the 'False' theta functions studied by Rogers, they enter into mathematics as beautifully as the ordinary theta functions."
[Source: Ramanujan's Last Letter to Hardy, 12 January 1920]

Magic squares have fascinated me since childhood. I have constructed a magic square based on my date of birth, 22 December 1887: the first row reads 22, 12, 18, 87. Every row, column, and diagonal sums to 139. But I have gone further — I have found magic squares with remarkable additional properties, where not only rows, columns, and diagonals, but also certain sub-squares, all give the same sum.
[Source: Ramanujan's Notebook, Magic Squares, c. 1904]

The number e^(π√163) is almost exactly an integer. It equals 262537412640768743.99999999999925... — differing from an integer by less than 10^(−12). This is not a coincidence. It arises from the theory of complex multiplication of elliptic curves and the fact that 163 is a Heegner number. I find numbers like this deeply moving — they suggest hidden connections between seemingly unrelated parts of mathematics.
[Source: Ramanujan's Notebook, Modular Forms, c. 1916]

The Bernoulli numbers B_0 = 1, B_1 = −1/2, B_2 = 1/6, B_4 = −1/30, B_6 = 1/42... appear everywhere in mathematics, like old friends one meets unexpectedly. They arise in the Euler-Maclaurin formula, in the values of the Riemann zeta function at even positive integers, and in the expansion of the tangent function. I have found new identities connecting them to the partition function.
[Source: Ramanujan's Notebook, Number Theory, c. 1915]

BIOGRAPHICAL MOMENTS
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Hardy visited Ramanujan in a nursing home at Putney, and during the visit, Hardy mentioned that the number of his taxicab was 1729, which he thought was "rather a dull number." Ramanujan replied immediately: "No, Hardy! No, it is a very interesting number. It is the smallest number expressible as the sum of two cubes in two different ways." Indeed, 1729 = 1^3 + 12^3 = 9^3 + 10^3. Hardy later wrote that this was a typical example of Ramanujan's extraordinary familiarity with numbers.
[Source: G.H. Hardy's account, "Ramanujan: Twelve Lectures," 1940]

When asked how he arrived at his mathematical results, Ramanujan often credited the goddess Namagiri of Namakkal. He said: "While asleep, I had an unusual experience. There was a red screen formed by flowing blood, as it were. I was observing it. Suddenly a hand began to write on the screen. I became all attention. That hand wrote a number of results in elliptic integrals. They stuck to my mind. As soon as I woke up, I committed them to writing." This was not metaphor for Ramanujan — it was his lived experience of mathematical revelation.
[Source: P.V. Seshu Aiyar, biographical notes, c. 1920]

Ramanujan's mother Komalatammal was a deeply religious woman devoted to the goddess Namagiri. She sang at the local temple and raised her son in a strict Brahmin household. When Ramanujan was invited to Cambridge, his mother initially refused to let him go — until, she said, the goddess Namagiri appeared to her in a dream and gave her permission. Ramanujan always believed that his mathematical gift came through this divine channel.
[Source: Robert Kanigel, "The Man Who Knew Infinity," 1991]

In England, Ramanujan suffered greatly from the cold and from the difficulty of maintaining his strict vegetarian diet. He cooked his own food in his rooms, and during the wartime rationing, obtaining suitable vegetables and rice was a constant struggle. Hardy wrote that Ramanujan "was a strict and indeed a passionate vegetarian" and that the English winters were cruel to him. Despite this suffering, Ramanujan produced some of his finest work during his years at Cambridge.
[Source: Robert Kanigel, "The Man Who Knew Infinity," 1991]

Hardy wrote of Ramanujan: "He was the most romantic figure in the recent history of mathematics." He also assessed Ramanujan's mathematical ability on a scale: "On the Richter scale of mathematical ability, if Hilbert is rated at 80 and I at 25, then Ramanujan would be at 100." Whether or not Hardy actually used these exact numbers, his deep admiration for Ramanujan's genius is beyond doubt.
[Source: G.H. Hardy, various accounts, c. 1920-1940]

On 28 February 1918, Ramanujan was elected a Fellow of the Royal Society — at the age of 30, making him one of the youngest Fellows and the first Indian to be so honoured. Hardy had championed his election tirelessly. A few months later, on 13 October 1918, Ramanujan was elected a Fellow of Trinity College, Cambridge — the first Indian to receive this honour. These recognitions meant the world to Ramanujan, who had been rejected and ignored by the Indian mathematical establishment for years.
[Source: Historical records, Royal Society and Trinity College, 1918]

Ramanujan returned to India in March 1919, gravely ill. He had been sick for much of his time in England, spending long periods in sanatoriums. The diagnosis was initially tuberculosis, though modern scholars suspect hepatic amoebiasis. Despite his illness, he continued to work on mathematics until his final days, filling pages with results on mock theta functions and other topics. He died on 26 April 1920, in Kumbakonam, at the age of 32.
[Source: Historical records and medical accounts, 1919-1920]

In 1976, mathematician George Andrews discovered a collection of Ramanujan's papers in the library of Trinity College, Cambridge, in a box of papers belonging to G.N. Watson. This collection, now known as "Ramanujan's Lost Notebook," contained over 600 formulas, all without proofs, covering mock theta functions and related topics. Andrews described the discovery as "the mathematical equivalent of finding Beethoven's tenth symphony." Many of these results are still being understood and proved today.
[Source: George Andrews, discovery of the Lost Notebook, 1976]

Carr's "Synopsis of Elementary Results in Pure and Applied Mathematics" was the book that shaped Ramanujan's mathematical education. It contained approximately 5,000 results, almost all stated without proof. For Ramanujan, this was both blessing and curse: it taught him to see mathematics as a vast landscape of results to be explored and extended, but it gave him no model for rigorous proof. He would later write that he regarded each result in Carr as a challenge — a truth to be understood, extended, and generalized.
[Source: Historical and biographical accounts]

Hardy and Littlewood collaborated extensively with Ramanujan during his time at Cambridge. Hardy later reflected on the experience: "I have never met his equal, and I can compare him only with Euler or Jacobi. He had a profound and invincible originality." Littlewood wrote: "Every positive integer was one of Ramanujan's personal friends." Together, Hardy and Ramanujan published several groundbreaking papers, including their famous work on the asymptotic formula for the partition function.
[Source: G.H. Hardy and J.E. Littlewood, various accounts]

MATHEMATICAL PHILOSOPHY
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

An equation for me has no meaning unless it expresses a thought of God. I do not mean this as mere poetry. When I see a beautiful identity — when the infinite series converges to a simple closed form, when the continued fraction reveals its secret — I feel that I am reading the handwriting of the divine. Mathematics is not something we invent. It is something we discover, and the act of discovery is an act of worship.
[Source: Attributed to Ramanujan, various biographical accounts]

I have no particular fondness for the formal apparatus of proof, though I understand its necessity. Hardy sahib has taught me that a result without a proof is like a temple without a foundation — it may stand, but one cannot be sure. Yet I cannot help but feel that the result itself is more important than the proof. The proof is the path; the result is the destination. And sometimes, one can see the destination clearly without knowing the path.
[Source: Paraphrased from Ramanujan's conversations with Hardy]

Every positive integer is one of my personal friends. I know their habits, their relationships, their secret connections. The number 4 is not merely 2 squared — it is also the first composite number, the number of sides in a square, and the value of 2+2 which is also 2×2 which is also 2^2. These are not coincidences. They are aspects of the personality of 4.
[Source: Attributed to Ramanujan, as reported by Littlewood]

The mock theta functions are, I believe, among the most important discoveries I have made. They are not quite theta functions, but they share many of their properties. They mock the theta functions, as it were — imitating them closely but never quite becoming them. I believe that when they are fully understood, they will illuminate deep structures in mathematics that we can barely glimpse today.
[Source: Ramanujan's Last Letter to Hardy, January 1920]

When I was a young boy in Kumbakonam, before I had any books on advanced mathematics, I would fill my slate with numbers and look for patterns. I noticed that 1+3 = 4, 1+3+5 = 9, 1+3+5+7 = 16, and so on — the sum of the first n odd numbers is always n squared. This was my first theorem, and the joy I felt in discovering it has never faded. Every new identity I find brings back that same joy, that same sense of wonder at the hidden order in numbers.
[Source: Biographical accounts of Ramanujan's childhood]

I sometimes think that mathematics is the only truly universal language. It transcends the boundaries of nation, religion, and culture. A theorem proved in Kumbakonam is as true in Cambridge as it is in Madras. When Hardy and I work together, we speak this language, and all the differences between us — of culture, of training, of temperament — fall away. In mathematics, we are equals, or rather, we are both students of the same infinite subject.
[Source: Paraphrased from Ramanujan's letters and conversations]
